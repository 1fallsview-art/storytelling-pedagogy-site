About this Project

This website explores storytelling as an important Indigenous approach to teaching and learning. In many Indigenous cultures, stories are not simply narratives for entertainment; they are ways of sharing knowledge, passing on cultural teachings, and strengthening relationships between people, communities, and the land.

Storytelling has long been used to support learning across generations and to communicate cultural values, identity, and ways of understanding the world. Indigenous storytelling practices emphasize holistic learning that engages the heart, mind, body, and spirit.

The goal of this website is to introduce key ideas about storytelling as pedagogy and to explore how educators can approach storytelling respectfully and thoughtfully in educational settings.

Writing guidance for authors: This page should explain the purpose of the website and provide background context for the topic. It should briefly introduce why storytelling is significant in Indigenous educational traditions and outline the themes explored in the different sections of the site.

Purpose of the Website

The purpose of this project is to explore storytelling as an educational practice and to highlight how Indigenous storytelling traditions can inform contemporary teaching and learning.

  • Introduce storytelling as a meaningful way of sharing knowledge.
  • Explain how storytelling can support learning and cultural understanding.
  • Discuss respectful engagement with Indigenous knowledge and traditions.
  • Provide examples of possible classroom applications.
  • Encourage reflection on the responsibilities of educators when working with Indigenous stories.

Organization of the Website

  • Teaching Through Story – introduces storytelling traditions and their role in learning.
  • Storytelling as Pedagogy – discusses educational perspectives on storytelling.
  • Respectful Practice – explores ethical considerations when engaging with Indigenous knowledge.
  • Applications – presents possible classroom or early childhood activities inspired by storytelling.
  • Reflection – invites educators to reflect on their own teaching practices.
  • Resources and Bibliography – includes scholarly sources for further learning.

Suggested Scholarly Sources

The following peer-reviewed sources provide important perspectives on Indigenous knowledge, storytelling, and education.

  • Archibald, J. (2008). Indigenous storywork: Educating the heart, mind, body, and spirit. Canadian Journal of Native Education, 31(1), 72–92.
  • Battiste, M. (2002). Indigenous knowledge and pedagogy in First Nations education: A literature review with recommendations. Canadian Journal of Native Education, 26(2), 144–160.
  • Iseke, J. (2013). Indigenous storytelling as research. International Review of Qualitative Research, 6(4), 559–577.
  • Simpson, L. B. (2014). Land as pedagogy: Nishnaabeg intelligence and rebellious transformation. Decolonization: Indigeneity, Education & Society, 3(3), 1–25.
  • Wilson, S. (2001). What is Indigenous research methodology? Canadian Journal of Native Education, 25(2), 175–179.
