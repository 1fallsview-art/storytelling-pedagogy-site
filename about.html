About this Project

This website explores storytelling as an Indigenous approach to teaching and learning. In many Indigenous traditions, stories are not simply narratives for entertainment; they are ways of sharing knowledge, communicating values, and strengthening relationships between people, communities, and the land.

Storytelling has long supported learning across generations and has helped transmit cultural teachings, identity, memory, and responsibility. This project examines how storytelling can be understood as pedagogy and how educators can approach Indigenous stories in respectful and thoughtful ways.

The purpose of this website is to introduce key ideas about storytelling, pedagogy, respectful practice, classroom applications, and reflection. It is designed as an educational resource for exploring how storytelling can shape teaching and learning.

Writing guidance for authors: This page should explain the purpose of the website, provide background context for the topic, and introduce why storytelling matters in Indigenous educational traditions. It should also help readers understand how the main sections of the website are organized.

Purpose of the Website

This project aims to explore storytelling as a meaningful educational practice and to show how Indigenous storytelling traditions can inform teaching and learning in contemporary educational settings.

  • Introduce storytelling as a way of knowing, learning, and sharing knowledge.
  • Explain how storytelling can support identity, belonging, and cultural understanding.
  • Highlight the importance of respectful engagement with Indigenous knowledge.
  • Provide possible classroom and early childhood applications.
  • Encourage reflection on the responsibilities of educators.

Organization of the Website

  • Teaching Through Story – introduces storytelling traditions and their role in learning.
  • Storytelling as Pedagogy – discusses educational perspectives on storytelling and storywork.
  • Respectful Practice – explores ethical and respectful engagement with Indigenous knowledge and stories.
  • Applications – presents possible classroom and early childhood learning ideas.
  • Reflection – invites educators to reflect on their own responsibilities and practice.
  • Resources and Bibliography – provides sources for further learning and research.
