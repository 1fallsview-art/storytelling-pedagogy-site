Classroom & Early Childhood Applications

Storytelling can be thoughtfully woven into everyday classroom and early childhood practice through books, materials, music, discussion, and experiences connected to the land. In these settings, storytelling is not treated as a simple teaching tool, but as a relational and meaningful way of learning with children.

These applications emphasize listening, reflection, creativity, and connection. Children are invited to engage with stories in different ways and to make meaningful links between stories, their experiences, their communities, and the natural world.

Using Storytelling in the Classroom

Storytelling is part of everyday learning. Classroom materials can support children in exploring stories, sharing ideas, and building meaningful connections to the world around them. Through books, open-ended materials, music, and nature-based invitations, children can revisit stories, represent their thinking, and participate in ways that are developmentally appropriate and inclusive.

Storytelling Through Books

Picture books can open space for conversations about identity, nature, relationships, and belonging. Books such as Benjamin Grows a Garden and I Am Connected invite children to think about caring for the land, noticing connections, and reflecting on their own experiences.

These stories can be shared through read-alouds, small-group conversations, or quieter reflective moments. Rather than focusing on one correct interpretation, educators can encourage children to wonder, respond, and make personal connections.

Animal Figurines and Storybooks

Animal figurines can be used alongside storybooks to help children retell stories, explore relationships, and represent ideas in concrete ways. As children move the figures and create scenes, they can revisit important moments in a story and express their understanding through play.

Wooden Storytelling Pieces

Wooden storytelling pieces, including talking sticks and other visual or cultural materials, can support storytelling, listening, and shared discussion. These materials can encourage children to take turns, tell their own stories, and participate respectfully in group learning experiences.

Puzzles and Visual Materials

Puzzles and other visual materials can help children build stories, notice details, and strengthen observation skills. Images of animals, landscapes, and natural elements can prompt children to describe what they see, make predictions, and create their own storylines.

Image provided by Melody Barrera

Storytelling Through Music and Listening

Audio materials such as CDs and recorded songs can support storytelling through listening, reflection, rhythm, and movement. Children may respond to these experiences by talking, singing, dancing, drawing, or acting out ideas from a story.

This creates additional entry points for participation and allows storytelling to be experienced in ways that are engaging, multimodal, and meaningful for different learners.

Image provided by Melody Barrera

Connecting to Land and Nature

Natural materials such as pinecones, wood, shells, corn, leaves, and stones can support storytelling experiences connected to the land. These materials invite children to explore texture, pattern, shape, and story while reflecting on relationships with the natural world.

In this kind of learning invitation, children can build scenes, retell events, and create their own stories using materials gathered from nature. These experiences can support curiosity, care, and respect for the land and living things.

Image provided by Melody Barrera

Supporting Curiosity, Reflection, and Discussion

Storytelling can support children’s curiosity by inviting them to ask questions, share ideas, and connect stories to their own lives. Children may reflect on experiences with nature, family, friendship, and community as they respond to stories in individual and shared ways.

Educators can encourage this process by creating space for conversation, listening carefully, and valuing children’s interpretations. Storytelling becomes a way of building relationships and making meaning together, rather than simply recalling facts.

Inclusive and Accessible Participation

Children can participate in storytelling in many different ways, including dramatic play, drawing, creating, movement, dance, verbal storytelling, and discussion. Offering multiple ways to listen, respond, and represent ideas helps create an inclusive and welcoming environment for all learners.

These flexible approaches support participation across different ages, strengths, communication styles, and learning preferences while keeping storytelling grounded in relationship, reflection, and respect.

Key Takeaway

Storytelling supports children’s learning by helping them build relationships, express their ideas, and make connections to the land, community, and one another in meaningful and respectful ways.
