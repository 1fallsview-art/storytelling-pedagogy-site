Classroom & Early Childhood Applications

Storytelling can be thoughtfully woven into everyday classroom and early childhood practice through books, materials, music, discussion, and experiences connected to the land. In these settings, storytelling is not treated as a simple teaching tool, but as a relational and meaningful way of learning with children, grounded in Indigenous pedagogies that emphasize connection, reflection, and responsibility (Archibald, 2008; see References).

These applications reflect storytelling as a relational and reflective process, where listening, respect, and connection to land and community are central to learning (Archibald, 2008).

These applications emphasize listening, reflection, creativity, and connection. Children are invited to engage with stories in different ways and to make meaningful links between stories, their experiences, their communities, and the natural world.

Storytelling Through Books

Picture books can open space for conversations about identity, nature, relationships, belonging, and care for the land. Books such as Benjamin Grows a Garden, I Am Connected, Birdsong, The Water Walker, When We Were Alone, and We Are Water Protectors invite children to think about connection, responsibility, community, and their own lived experiences.

These stories can be shared through read-alouds, small-group conversations, or quieter reflective moments. Rather than focusing on a single correct interpretation, educators can encourage children to wonder, respond, and make personal connections, supporting meaning-making and relational learning (Serafini, 2013; see References).

Educators play an important role by guiding discussions, asking open-ended questions, and supporting children in making connections between stories and their own lives.

Benjamin Grows a Garden
Melanie Florence (Author), Hawlii Pichette (Illustrator)

A story about gardening, growth, and caring for the land that invites children to think about relationships between people, plants, and place.

This book encourages children to reflect on connection, belonging, and the ways people, land, and living things are linked to one another.

Birdsong

An intergenerational story about friendship, change, and seasonal rhythms that supports reflection on care, relationships, and belonging.

The Water Walker
Joanne Robertson (Written and illustrated by)

This story shares the work of water protection and invites children to think about responsibility, perseverance, and respectful relationships with water and the land.

When We Were Alone
David A. Robertson (Author), Julie Flett (Illustrator)

A gentle intergenerational story that introduces children to themes of identity, culture, history, and resilience through a conversation between a child and her grandmother.

We Are Water Protectors
Carole Lindstrom (Author), Michaela Goade (Illustrator)

A powerful story about protecting water that encourages children to reflect on interconnectedness, environmental care, and collective responsibility.

How these books can be used: These books can be shared through read-alouds, small-group conversations, and reflective discussion. Educators can invite children to make personal connections, ask questions, respond through drawing or storytelling, and explore themes such as identity, relationships, belonging, and caring for the land and water.

Animal Figurines and Storybooks

Animal figurines can be used alongside storybooks to support children in retelling stories, engaging in symbolic play, and representing ideas in concrete ways, as manipulatives and props have been shown to enhance narrative recall, language development, and representational thinking (Morrow, 1985; Vygotsky, 1978; see References). Through hands-on interaction, children can revisit story events, experiment with different perspectives, and express their understanding through play. These experiences support meaning-making and allow children to engage with stories in ways that are active, relational, and personally meaningful.

Example: After reading a story, children might use animal figurines to retell key moments and then share their version with others in a small group.

Educators might ask: What is happening in your story? How are the characters helping each other? What might happen next?

Wooden Storytelling Pieces

Wooden storytelling materials such as talking sticks, drums, and figures can support listening, reflection, and relational learning, reflecting Indigenous storytelling approaches that emphasize respect, responsibility, and connection (Archibald, 2008; see References). These materials can help create structured yet flexible spaces where children take turns, share ideas, and listen to others with care. In this way, storytelling becomes a shared and respectful process, where each voice is valued and learning is shaped through relationships and interaction.

Example: A talking stick can be used during circle time so each child has a turn to speak while others listen without interruption.

Educators might ask: What would you like to share? What did you notice when you were listening?

Puzzles and Visual Materials

Visual storytelling materials can support observation, interpretation, and multiple ways of expression, aligning with Universal Design for Learning principles that emphasize flexible and varied ways for children to engage with and represent ideas (CAST, 2024; see References). Through images, puzzles, or story stones, children can construct and communicate ideas using visual and symbolic representations. This allows for diverse entry points into storytelling, supporting children with different communication styles and encouraging creative and flexible thinking.

Example: Children might use story stones or puzzle images to build a shared story.

Educators might ask: What do you see in this image? What could this part of the story mean?

Storytelling Through Music and Listening

Listening experiences can support storytelling through reflection, movement, and multimodal engagement, aligning with Universal Design for Learning principles that emphasize flexible and varied ways for children to engage with and express ideas (CAST, 2024; see References). Music and sound can invite children to imagine mood, setting, and emotion in ways that extend beyond spoken language alone. This helps create inclusive opportunities for children to participate through listening, drawing, movement, and oral sharing.

Example: Children listen to music and imagine a story, then share or express it through drawing or movement.

Educators might ask: What kind of story did you imagine? How did the music make you feel?

Connecting to Land and Nature

Natural materials can support storytelling connected to relationships with the land, reflecting Indigenous storytelling approaches that emphasize respect, responsibility, and connection (Archibald, 2008; see References). By exploring items such as leaves, seeds, and stones, children can begin to notice patterns, cycles, and connections within the natural world. These experiences encourage respect, curiosity, and a sense of responsibility, positioning the land as an active and meaningful part of storytelling and learning.

Example: Children explore items such as seeds, pinecones, and stones and discuss their roles in nature.

Educators might ask: Where might this come from? What does it do? How is it connected to the land?

Key Takeaway

Storytelling supports children’s learning by helping them build relationships, express their ideas, and make connections to the land, community, and each other in meaningful and respectful ways.
