Classroom & Early Childhood Applications

Storytelling can be thoughtfully woven into everyday classroom and early childhood practice through books, materials, music, discussion, and experiences connected to the land. In these settings, storytelling is not treated as a simple teaching tool, but as a relational and meaningful way of learning with children, grounded in Indigenous pedagogies that emphasize connection, reflection, and responsibility (Archibald, 2008; see References).

These applications reflect storytelling as a relational and reflective process, where listening, respect, and connection to land and community are central to learning (Archibald, 2008).

These applications emphasize listening, reflection, creativity, and connection. Children are invited to engage with stories in different ways and to make meaningful links between stories, their experiences, their communities, and the natural world.

Storytelling Through Books

Picture books can open space for conversations about identity, nature, relationships, belonging, and care for the land. These stories invite children to reflect on connection, responsibility, community, and their own lived experiences.

Rather than focusing on a single correct interpretation, educators can encourage children to wonder, respond, and make personal connections, supporting meaning-making and relational learning (Serafini, 2013; see References).

Benjamin Grows a Garden
Melanie Florence, Hawlii Pichette

A story about gardening and caring for the land, highlighting relationships between people, plants, and place.

I Am Connected
Ḵung Jaadee, Carla Joseph

Encourages children to reflect on connection, belonging, and relationships between people, land, and living things.

Birdsong
Julie Flett

An intergenerational story exploring friendship, change, and seasonal rhythms, supporting reflection on relationships and care.

The Water Walker
Joanne Robertson

Highlights the importance of protecting water and invites children to consider responsibility and relationships with the land.

When We Were Alone
David A. Robertson, Julie Flett

A gentle story about identity, culture, and resilience, shared through an intergenerational conversation.

We Are Water Protectors
Carole Lindstrom, Michaela Goade

Encourages reflection on environmental care, interconnectedness, and collective responsibility.

How these books can be used: These books can be shared through read-alouds, small-group conversations, and reflective discussion. Educators can invite children to make personal connections, ask questions, respond through drawing or storytelling, and explore themes such as identity, relationships, belonging, and caring for the land and water.
