Teaching Through Story

Introduce storytelling as a way of teaching and learning within Indigenous knowledge systems. Explain how stories can carry knowledge, values, relationships, and responsibilities, rather than functioning only as entertainment or classroom content.

Consider how learning through story involves listening, reflection, and interpretation. You may explore how meaning can change depending on context, time, place, and the experiences of the listener.

Describe how storytelling shifts the role of the educator from delivering information to supporting shared meaning-making and relational learning.

What Stories Can Carry

Knowledge and Relationships

  • Explain how stories can carry teachings about relationships among people, land, and more-than-human life.
  • Consider how stories hold community memory, history, and identity.
  • Explore the role of language, humour, and cultural continuity.
  • Discuss how stories may include ethical teachings about how to live well with others.

Respectful Engagement

  • Explain that stories come from specific Nations and communities.
  • Discuss how meaning may depend on context, listeners, time, and place.
  • Emphasize the importance of approaching stories with care, humility, and responsibility.
  • Avoid presenting stories as having a single fixed “moral” or universal meaning.

Teaching Considerations

Reflect on how storytelling can be used in educational settings while maintaining respect for cultural context and community ownership.

Consider what responsibilities educators have when engaging with stories, including questions of permission, representation, and relational accountability.

You may also connect storytelling to broader ideas about inclusive, relational, and meaningful teaching practices.
