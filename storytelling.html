Teaching Through Story

Storytelling is a powerful way of teaching and learning within Indigenous knowledge systems. Rather than serving only as entertainment, stories carry knowledge, values, relationships, and responsibilities that support meaningful and holistic learning.

Through storytelling, learners are invited to listen, reflect, and interpret meaning over time. Stories do not always present a single, fixed message; instead, their meanings emerge through context, lived experience, and relationships with others and the Land. This process encourages learners to think deeply, make connections, and engage with knowledge in a relational way.

In teaching practice, storytelling shifts the role of the educator. Rather than delivering information, educators create space for dialogue, reflection, and shared meaning-making. Learning becomes a collaborative process in which both educators and learners contribute to understanding.

What Stories Can Carry

Knowledge and Relationships

  • Teachings about relationships among people, land, and more-than-human life.
  • Community memory, history, and identity.
  • Language, humour, and cultural continuity.
  • Ethical teachings about how to live well with others.

Respectful Engagement

  • Recognizing that stories come from specific Nations and communities.
  • Understanding that meanings may change depending on context, listeners, time, and place.
  • Approaching stories with care, humility, and responsibility.
  • Avoiding the reduction of stories to a single “moral” or fixed interpretation.

Teaching Considerations

When using storytelling in educational settings, it is important to recognize that stories are not simply classroom materials. They are connected to cultural knowledge, community relationships, and responsibilities. Educators should avoid treating stories as content that can be taken out of context or used without permission.

Teaching through story involves listening, patience, and respect. It encourages educators to consider whose knowledge is being shared, how it is shared, and what responsibilities come with engaging in that knowledge.

This approach supports inclusive and meaningful learning environments by valuing multiple perspectives and honoring the relational nature of knowledge.
