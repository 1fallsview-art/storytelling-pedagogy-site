Reflection

Learning about Indigenous storytelling as pedagogy invites educators to reconsider some common assumptions about teaching and learning. In many educational settings, teaching is often viewed primarily as the transmission of information. However, Indigenous storytelling traditions emphasize that learning occurs through relationships, listening, reflection, and shared meaning-making. Stories are not simply narratives to be consumed; they carry cultural knowledge, values, and responsibilities that unfold through thoughtful engagement.

From this perspective, storytelling encourages educators to move beyond viewing knowledge as fixed information delivered by a teacher. Instead, learning can be understood as a relational process that connects people, place, culture, and experience. Stories may invite listeners to reflect, interpret meaning in their own time, and revisit ideas through conversation and community dialogue. This approach highlights the importance of patience, humility, and openness in educational practice.

The artwork above illustrates ideas that resonate with storytelling as pedagogy. The flowing shapes, natural landscape, and presence of animals suggest connection, balance, and continuity within the natural world. In many storytelling traditions, stories help people understand their place within these relationships. Rather than presenting knowledge as isolated facts, stories may emphasize interconnectedness among humans, animals, land, and community. For educators, this perspective can encourage teaching approaches that value reflection, dialogue, and respect for multiple ways of knowing.

Engaging Indigenous stories in educational contexts also raises important responsibilities for educators, particularly for those who are not Indigenous. Stories should not be treated simply as teaching tools or classroom materials. Instead, educators should approach them with respect for cultural context, community knowledge, and appropriate protocols. This may involve learning about the origins of stories, acknowledging the communities and traditions from which they come, and recognizing that some knowledge is shared only in particular cultural settings.

Reflection on storytelling pedagogy therefore encourages ongoing learning and humility. Educators can ask themselves how their teaching practices might support deeper listening, relational understanding, and meaningful engagement with diverse perspectives. Rather than viewing reflection as a final step, it becomes an ongoing process that shapes how educators learn from stories, from communities, and from their students.

Questions for reflection

For educators

  • How can teaching move beyond delivering information toward building relationships?
  • What does it mean to listen carefully and respectfully in learning contexts?
  • How can educators approach stories as sources of cultural knowledge rather than simple classroom materials?

For practice

  • How can learning environments support reflection, dialogue, and meaning-making?
  • What responsibilities do non-Indigenous educators have when engaging Indigenous perspectives?
  • How can educators continue learning from communities and ongoing professional reflection?

Continuing the learning

Reflection is not a single activity but an ongoing process. Educators can continue developing their understanding of Indigenous storytelling and pedagogy by reading scholarly work, engaging with community knowledge where appropriate, and reflecting on how teaching practices shape students’ experiences of learning and belonging.
