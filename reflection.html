Relational Reflection

Engaging with Indigenous storytelling as pedagogy requires educators to rethink foundational assumptions about the nature of knowledge. Rather than viewing teaching as the "delivery" of information, Storywork highlights learning as a relational process shaped by active listening, patience, and shared meaning-making.

This shift reminds us that knowledge is not a static commodity but a living relationship between people, community, and the Land. Within this framework, meaning is developed gradually through Dadirri (deep listening) and dialogue rather than immediate explanation. For educators, this necessitates a stance of cultural humility and a commitment to "nourishing the learning spirit" (Battiste, 2013).

Reflection on storytelling pedagogy also centers the concept of Responsibility. Indigenous stories are not merely classroom "materials"; they are cultural belongings. Educators, particularly those who are non-Indigenous, must approach these perspectives as witnesses rather than owners, ensuring that cultural protocols and local contexts are respected (Kovach, 2009).

Critical Questions for Practice

The Educator’s Journey

  • How can I move from being an "instructor" to a "witness" of Indigenous knowledge?
  • In what ways am I practicing Dadirri to listen with my heart as well as my mind?
  • Am I treating stories as "decorative additions" or as central, holistic pedagogical frameworks?

The Four R's in Action

  • Reciprocity: How am I giving back to the communities whose stories I am sharing?
  • Respect: Have I sought permission or followed local protocols for these specific narratives?
  • Relevance: How does this story connect the learner to their specific Land and community?

Continuing the Learning

Reflection is not a final step, but a continuous cycle of Storywork. Educators can deepen their practice by moving beyond theory and toward respectful engagement with local Elders and Knowledge Keepers. By recognizing that teaching practices shape a student’s sense of belonging and identity, we commit to an ongoing journey of decolonizing the classroom (Gerlach, 2018).
