Reflection (draft)

Exploring storytelling as an Indigenous approach to teaching and learning encourages educators to reflect on how knowledge is shared, how relationships are built, and how learning can be grounded in respect, listening, and community. This page can be used to gather reflective ideas about what educators may learn from storytelling and what responsibilities come with engaging these approaches in educational settings.

Draft focus: This page is a placeholder for reflective ideas, educator learning, and implications for practice. Replace these general notes with course concepts, academic sources, and group analysis later.

Questions for reflection

For educators

  • How can teaching move beyond delivering information toward building relationships?
  • What does it mean to listen carefully and respectfully in learning contexts?
  • How can educators avoid treating Indigenous stories as simple classroom materials?

For practice

  • How can classroom environments support reflection, dialogue, and multiple ways of meaning-making?
  • What responsibilities do non-Indigenous educators have when engaging Indigenous perspectives?
  • How can educators continue learning from community guidance and local contexts?

Implications for teaching and learning

  • Learning can be understood as relational, reflective, and connected to community.
  • Stories may support belonging, identity, responsibility, and ethical understanding.
  • Educators may need ongoing reflection to engage Indigenous approaches respectfully and thoughtfully.
